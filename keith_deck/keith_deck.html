First Principles
The team you build is the company you build.
Vinod Khosla → Keith Rabois → every founder who listened

People get distracted by market, customers, product, technology. Ultimately it's a team. Right people → everything else becomes easy. Wrong people → everything else becomes hard.

If a founder shows the ability early in her career to assess talent ruthlessly and accurately, she can go very far with no other abilities whatsoever.

Hard Truths // Keith Rabois 04 / 30
Origin Story

Why was PayPal the
generator of generations?

Peter Thiel and Max Levchin marshalled an incredible density of talent at PayPal — ~254 people in Mountain View when acquired. Within that, 12–17 people who would go on to build Palantir, Tesla, YouTube, LinkedIn, Yelp, and dozens more.

Their recruiting model: first- or second-degree connections only. Not scalable for everyone — but if your network has unique talent, there's no substitute for referral density.

Lesson 1
Keith was mediocre at hiring strangers early on. He succeeded by recruiting internally — finding undertapped people already in the building.
Lesson 2
He could identify talent in people he'd worked alongside — but not in 30-minute interviews with strangers. That took years of training.
1 + 1 = 3
David Sacks's rule: for every incremental hire you must show disproportionate, nonlinear returns. Otherwise you have no leadership leverage.
Hard Truths // Keith Rabois 05 / 30
How to Get Better

Hiring is a muscle.
You need to exercise it.

  • Relentless referencing. Tony at DoorDash does 20 references on every senior hire. 20. It's tedious and it works.
  • Ask the right question. Not "Was Max a good employee?" — that gets a mixed answer. Ask: "Is Max capable of being a world-class entrepreneur?" Completely different signal.
  • 30-day loop. Ask yourself 30 days after every hire: "Would I make this decision again?" Research shows this is as predictive as waiting a full year.
  • Reference arc. Ask referees: "What would lead to this person's greatest success? And if something went wrong, what would be the primary root cause?" Two arcs. Enormous insight.
The Senior Interview Question
"If you were CEO of [their company], what would you have done differently?"

Follow-up gold: "Why weren't you able to persuade [CEO] to do it?"

Tests strategic mindset and internal influence simultaneously.
Top-10 basis points can't be taught — only exercised
Hard Truths // Keith Rabois 06 / 30
The Framework
Hiring more people without adding barrels just stacks people behind the same initiatives.
Keith Rabois — the diagnosis behind every CEO's "we hired a lot but get less done" complaint
Barrels
People who can independently drive an initiative from inception to success. They accumulate resources, motivate people, measure impact, and deliver. Fire and forget.
Ammunition
Everyone else — skilled contributors who execute inside an initiative someone else is driving. Valuable. Not the bottleneck. Adding more doesn't create new tracks.
Hard Truths // Keith Rabois 07 / 30
Barrels / The Numbers

Your parallel initiative capacity = your barrel count.

PayPal
12–17 barrels / 254 people
OpenAI
~2 barrels (Sam Altman's estimate)
Typical
1–3 at most companies

The math: 15 barrels = 15 things you can do simultaneously. Hiring 50 more people without finding new barrels? You still have 15 parallel tracks. You just added coordination tax and drag coefficient.

The ratio of barrels to ammunition is what dictates the number of important initiatives that can be pursued simultaneously. If you need to do more, you need more barrels — not more headcount.

Hard Truths // Keith Rabois 08 / 30
Identifying Barrels

Can they take that hill?

"There's a hill over there. We're going up that hill. One way or another, they will motivate people, accumulate resources, measure what they need, and get your company across that hill. That's a barrel. Anything less is not a barrel."

  • Agency over their domain — they own the outcome, not the process
  • Proactive communication — returns with issues identified, root causes diagnosed, attempts already made, and sufficient time for you to help
  • Scope expansion — can handle progressively harder problems as trust accumulates
  • The smoothie test — Taylor Francis, intern, day 2: Keith couldn't get cold smoothies delivered at 9pm for months. Taylor solved it in a day. "I found a barrel."

Most people hearing this assume they are barrels. Most people are wrong.

Fire & Forget
A true barrel takes something off your plate completely. The outcome can be narrow at first — the scope expands as trust compounds. What never varies: they will deliver.
Hard Truths // Keith Rabois 09 / 30
The Strategy

Build your company on undiscovered talent.

Peter Thiel taught Keith this on day one: the way to build a company against large incumbents with infinite money is to find people the black-box machine mis-processes. That's been Keith's crusade for 25 years.

The salary cap analogy: As a startup, you have one-tenth the salary cap of your competitors. Competing for the same proven talent is a losing game. Find the alpha elsewhere.

The secret: understand why a large company's evaluation process will fail this person. If you can isolate the mis-processing reason, you've found your edge.

Why younger skews work
Not because you need young people — but because they have less data. By 30+, you're a known quantity. Less data = harder for homogeneous evaluation machines to process = more alpha.
The credit score analogy
Employment evaluation works like FICO. By 35, there are so many data points that the system processes you like everyone else. No data points = system fails = opportunity.
The key question
"What would Meta/Google miss about this person, and why?" That gap is your hiring edge.
Hard Truths // Keith Rabois 10 / 30
Attraction

The pitch that actually works for proven talent.

Mission and vision are table stakes. What actually moves great talent is more specific: convincing them that their particular skill is the critical blocker to the company's current success. They're not joining a company — they're betting on themselves.

This is how Keith ended up at Square in 2010 — "There are only two or three people in the world who know both financial services and the startup world. You're one of them." He left Google after two weeks.

The Frame
"Not only is this a great company building something cool — your specific ability is uniquely differentiated, and you can solve this blocker." Impact + ego + betting on yourself.

This also explains why companies developing talent internally beat those hiring "senior experienced" people. Internal builders are betting on themselves; external senior hires are value-preserving.

Value Creation vs. Value Preservation → hire for the former, always.
Hard Truths // Keith Rabois 11 / 30
Operating Principle
The single role of the CEO is offsetting complacency. The better you're doing, the more you should push.
Keith Rabois — channeling Mike Mortiz's "relentless application of force"

The more success you have, the more complacent the organization tends to get. And unless you've built a true network effect, you cannot afford to coast. When a company is struggling, be supportive. When a company is thriving, be critical — isolate the problems that will become critical while everyone is borderline complacent.

Really talented people are like superb athletes. When things are going well and people are coasting, they're not happy. Morale actually goes down for the best people when things are skating.

Lenny confirmed this with Brian Chesky: "When we had little breaks, morale went down. People didn't know what they were working on." Pressure is a feature, not a bug, for elite talent.

I hate talking to customers.
I refuse to allow colleagues of mine to talk to customers.
Keith Rabois — for consumer and SMB products

Consumer behavior is subconscious. When you force a conscious answer to a subconscious decision, you get misleading information — even when the customer is trying to be honest. Ask someone why they bought a Porsche. They'll tell you every reason except the real one.

Hard Truths // Keith Rabois 14 / 30
Contrarian Take #1 / Unpacked

Customer feedback: not just unhelpful —
actively harmful.

The moment you hear "I talked to eight customers and…" in a meeting, even if they acknowledge it's not statistically representative, the data gets anchored in everyone's brain. Every subsequent decision is contaminated. It's extraordinarily difficult to dislodge.

  • In the real world, your product must cut through the clutter of daily life. An isolated customer interview is not a proxy for that.
  • DoorDash wasn't discovered by asking people if they wanted a food delivery button. Tony and Evan walked in with one stat: 93% of US restaurants don't deliver.
  • The right metric is selling tickets — optimize for CAC/LTV loops, not survey sentiment.
When it DOES work
Enterprise with a defined set of must-win accounts. 30 accounts, 30 decision-makers, utilitarian decisions, real economic stakes. You can actually talk to all 30 and influence the CEO. That's valuable.

If you're targeting a billion people, you are not getting representative feedback.

"Everything important you need to learn about humans was written by Shakespeare. Just read Shakespeare. That's better than all the customer research."

Hard Truths // Keith Rabois 15 / 30
Contrarian Take #2

Criticize people in public,
not in private.

When you give negative feedback privately, you're optimizing for the atomic unit — not the system. The rest of the team sees the problem persisting and doesn't know it's being addressed. Their suspicions compound.

Public criticism lets colleagues: (a) see the issue is on the radar, (b) raise their hand to help, (c) learn what the bar actually is. It becomes a team-building exercise rather than individual shame.

The best sports coaches do a mix — but what they say in front of the team tends to matter more than the private sessions.

High performance machines don't have psychological safety. They're about winning.

Recommended reading: The Jordan Rules. If you want to build like Michael Jordan, you have to act like Michael Jordan managed his teammates.

Psych. safety negatively correlated with performance
Hard Truths // Keith Rabois 16 / 30
The AI Shift

The #1 AI token consumer in the best companies
isn't the CTO. It's the CMO.

At the most AI-forward companies Keith sits on the boards of, it isn't engineers leading AI adoption. It's the CMOs. They're intellectually curious, suddenly have tools to do things they always wanted, and don't need to rely on chains of deputies to produce real work product.

What they're building: analytics, campaigns, CEO-ready insights — shipping things or shipping drafts of things. They've become individual contributors again, at executive scale.

The future-proofing recipe
1. Be intellectually curious.
2. Learn new tools with your hands — don't delegate the learning.
3. Use AI to collapse the dependency chains between you and actual output.

You can work harder AND work smarter. But curiosity is the prerequisite.

Director of Engineering at Ramp ships as much code personally while managing a team of 20 as he did as an individual contributor — using AI as a second team of specialized contributors.

Hard Truths // Keith Rabois 17 / 30
The Triad in the AI Era

PM · Designer · Engineer — all becoming the same role.

PM
The role is dying — or evolving into CEO
Sequential year-long roadmaps are incoherent. Things that were impossible in November are easy in March. What makes sense: a PM who can exploit new capabilities this week and ship next week. The skill is now: what are we building and why?
Engineer
Commercial instincts become the premium
The best engineers Keith has worked with always had commercial instincts — Max Levchin, Jeremy Stoppelman. At the age of AI, engineers who understand business won't need large teams. They'll marshal AI as a second team.
Designer
Design + code are merging — alpha in storytelling
The number of open design roles has plateaued for 3 years. But the alpha is in design: cutting through the clutter with the right frame is the NP-hard problem. Shopify already requires all product presentations as working demos, not slides.

All three roles are converging on the same core skill: business acumen — understanding the company's equation, where it's going, and being able to create things that move the needle individually.

Hard Truths // Keith Rabois 18 / 30
PM Role / Deep Dive

The idea of a PM makes no sense in the future.

Decompose what a PM does: take inputs from customers, create a sequential organized roadmap for the next year. That world is — in Keith's word — "ridiculous." Foundation models improve so fast that a year-long roadmap is incoherent from day one.

  • Things impossible in November are easy in March. Plan accordingly.
  • High-growth companies will notice something is now possible this week and ship new value for customers next week.
  • Intermediary PMs as translators/schedulers don't fit this tempo.

The surviving PM skill isn't roadmap management. It's being a mini-CEO: knowing what to build, why, and being able to ship it yourself.

Shopify's Rule (2 years running)
No PM presentations in PowerPoint or Keynote. Every product discussion must be a workable demo. Execs refuse to look at static "we're going to have this feature" slides. Everything must be working.
Hard Truths // Keith Rabois 19 / 30
Operating Signal

The one thing world-class companies all share early: tempo.

Roloff Botha joined Square's board and six months in told Keith: "I haven't seen this kind of tempo since our PayPal days" — after 9 years as a VC. The signal: "At board meeting X, you identify an opportunity or problem. By the next board meeting, you've shipped solutions, addressed it, featured it. Constantly. Consistently."

Ramp on card shipping: Normally takes 9–12 months best case. Ramp was on the precipice in 3 months. Keith pre-empted the Series A after seeing that — before they'd shipped a single card.

The Ramp clock
Days.ramp.com: the number of days since launch. Every board meeting opens with the counter. Not a metric they track — a value they embody.
Fair's signal
Keith's chief of staff: "If there's one company that would cause me to leave VC, it's Fair. By the next morning's meeting — not next board meeting — they've identified root causes, shipped solutions, and measured impact."
The compounding effect
Speed at this level compounds. The critical density of talent gets better. The DM improves. It becomes an accumulating advantage that's very hard to replicate.
Hard Truths // Keith Rabois 20 / 30
Hiring Philosophy

The best companies skipped senior external hires and promoted from within.

Ramp, Trade Republic, and most of Keith's highest-performing portfolio companies have a common thread: they essentially don't hire senior experienced people for most functions. They absorb ambitious younger talent and develop them rapidly through osmosis.

The chief of staff-to-senior-leader pipeline: CMO was last chief of staff. Head of product is current chief of staff. A factory that trains leaders through proximity to decision-making.

Exception: roles that require value preservation (legal, compliance) may warrant experienced external hires. Value creation roles? Almost never.

Value Creation vs. Preservation
Hire experienced externals for value preservation. Grow internal talent for value creation. Most founders get this backwards — they hire senior VPs for the high-growth functions and develop internally only in operations and legal.

Why it works: internal talent bets on themselves, absorbs the company's specific context, and moves at the company's tempo — not a prior employer's. External senior hires often slow tempo while they calibrate.

Hard Truths // Keith Rabois 21 / 30
The Content Question

AI content surpassing human content
is inevitable.

In Chinese TikTok, it's all AI video now — and people actively enjoy it. Keith's prediction: content will sort into two poles. An algorithm pole (best content wins, AI or human irrelevant) and a curation pole (provenance premium, like owning a real Warhol vs. a high-fidelity replica).

The writing paradox: AI is called a "large language model" — trained entirely on text — yet AI-generated writing is the least impressive AI output. Keith's theory: token rationing forces compression that degrades long-form quality. Short, specific prompts produce dramatically better outputs.

The Brief-Writing Lesson
As a litigator, Keith would spend the first week of a 3-week brief just walking around thinking about the first paragraph. Once those three sentences clicked, he'd write 30 pages in 2 days.

AI writing works the same way: the frame still requires a human. Power-through works once you have it.

The alpha in marketing and design isn't the tools or the channels — it's the storytelling. "The proverbial thousand songs in your market" — only the person who finds the right frame is worth anything.

Hard Truths // Keith Rabois 22 / 30
Investor Framework

The existential question for every AI startup right now.

To build a successful startup, you need to build for 8–20 years into the future. The rate of progress at multiple foundation labs simultaneously raises real questions about whether even thriving companies can sustain their position.

  • Question 1: Are the foundation labs going to be so proficient there's no oxygen left? Can you survive the next decade of foundation model progress?
  • Question 2: What are the accumulating advantages? Over time, what makes this business better and better, easier and easier?
  • Doesn't need to be proven: Just articulated. Can the founder map when accumulating advantages would appear and how they'd leverage them?

Keith is a founder-driven investor. The only thing he cares about: "Does this founder have a non-zero chance of changing an industry or the world?" If yes — seed or Series A — he's in. No other questions needed.

The Ugly Baby Signal
He wants half his VC friends to laugh at his investments. If they laugh, it means real alpha. Ugly babies with no obvious pedigree — Airbnb, Palantir, early OpenAI — are exactly where the extraordinary returns live.
Hard Truths // Keith Rabois 23 / 30
Contrarian Take #3

Don't do post-mortems on failure
when the company is doing great.

In a recent board meeting, well-meaning board members proposed failure retrospectives. Keith pushed back: "If you overemphasize failures, people think they're going to get criticized. You deter people from taking ambitious shots on goal."

You want people to be excited about raising their hands for very difficult problems. That's how you create value. Cataloguing failures systematically works against that instinct.

This is where psychological safety has its one legitimate use: be bold, be ambitious, take the risk — don't worry about the failing unless there's a factual miss you demonstrably could have avoided.

Keith's investing hit rate: 30–40% for early stage is "golden by definition." 60–70% failure rate is the baseline. Investors who can't absorb that stop taking the shots that produce the outliers.

"Let's just focus on winning."

The Jordan/Federer frame: Jordan missed 109 game-winning shots. Federer wins 60% of his points — loses 40%. The best accept failure as a tax on taking the shots, not as a curriculum to dissect.

Hard Truths // Keith Rabois 25 / 30
Meta-Observation
"I don't have as many contrarian takes these days — they've all been proven right."
Keith Rabois — on the shelf-life of good ideas

The natural arc of a great contrarian idea: start with a contrarian take → stick to it → the world comes around → it becomes consensus. Like Airbnb: started as a laughable idea, became how half the world books accommodation. You want the inflection where your contrarian idea becomes consensus. But you have to survive the laughing phase.

Good contrarian ideas don't stay contrarian forever. That's the entire point. You're early, not wrong.

This creates a refresh-rate problem: Keith now has to generate new contrarian takes to stay ahead. The current one he's watching: AI companies that look healthy in the short term but won't survive 8 years of foundation model progress.

Hard Truths // Keith Rabois 26 / 30
Counterintuitive / Creation

Why the most original work happens without feedback.

Taylor Swift, in an acceptance speech Keith watched: she built her early craft in a room, alone, iterating without posting or sharing — thousands of hours. Her advice: find ways to not expose yourself to feedback for a long time. Today, that discipline is nearly impossible with social media.

Artists who had early un-feedback-driven success often struggle to recreate it. Once they have an audience, they get trapped by the data. The first success wasn't data-driven — it resonated on its own terms. Subsequent work becomes derivative because it optimizes for the audience rather than the creator's instinct.

The Ugly Baby Parallel
Every great idea at Pixar starts as an "ugly baby" — nobody wants to engage with it, nurture it, pay attention to it. Same with startups. The Craigslist listing data that convinced Keith on Airbnb: 30 people in the Bay Area had already typed "I want to rent someone's bedroom." Tiny. But the conviction it created was enormous.

His investment heuristic: "If I want half my VC friends to laugh at me — I'm in a good place. That's where the real alpha lives."

Closing / Field Notes

What to carry out of this room.

01 · Team
The team you build is the company you build
Everything else — market, product, technology — is downstream of who you hire and how you evaluate them. One barrel unlocks a new parallel initiative. One mis-hire adds drag coefficient to everything.
02 · Barrels
Count your barrels; that's your capacity
Hiring headcount without adding barrels just stacks people behind the same initiatives. If you need to do more, find people who can fire-and-forget — not people to manage those who can.
03 · Discovery
Build on undiscovered talent, always
The salary cap is real. Find the people the black-box machine mis-processes. Understand why they're being missed. That gap is your hiring moat — and it compounds.
04 · Pressure
Push harder when winning
Complacency is the enemy. Elite talent is unhappy when they're coasting — morale drops. The CEO's job is to offset complacency, which means being more critical precisely when things are going best.
05 · AI Era
The CMO, not the CTO, is winning at AI
Intellectual curiosity + willingness to use tools with your own hands = future-proof. The PM role is dying. Design and code are merging. Business acumen is the durable skill in the product triad.
06 · Contrarian
Be early, not wrong — then be right loudly
Good contrarian takes become consensus. The goal isn't to stay contrarian forever — it's to survive the laughing phase and be there when the world catches up. Ugly babies become empires.
