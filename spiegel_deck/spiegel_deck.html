The thesis

The product is the table stakes. The thing that’s hard — the thing that actually decides who wins in the AI era — is putting it in front of people who haven’t already downloaded six other versions of it.

// 04 — The thesis

Distribution is the new moat.

Software stopped being a moat 15 years ago. AI didn’t change that — it just made everyone else realize it. Whoever owns the channel wins the category.

Transcript Brief//Lessons
05 / 20
A lesson Snap learned the hard way

Every feature gets copied. Software alone has never been a moat.

Snap learned this 15 years ago, watching its inventions show up in competitors’ apps within months. That’s the same lesson the AI industry is learning right now.

Snap, 2013–2018

Stories. Filters. Lenses. Bitmoji.

  • Each one shipped first by Snap.
  • Each one cloned within 6–18 months.
  • The clones often shipped on bigger networks.
  • Conclusion: the feature was never the moat.

AI industry, today

Models. Agents. Copilots. Wrappers.

  • Capabilities replicate across providers in weeks.
  • Open-source closes most gaps within a season.
  • Pricing collapses faster than retention can be built.
  • The moat lives somewhere else — channel, brand, hardware, taste.

Transcript Brief//Velocity
10 / 20
Velocity

Day one, you present work.

Snap’s design culture is unromantic about process. The expectation isn’t a deck about what you might do — it’s the thing itself, in front of the room.

  1. Make things, don’t talk about making things.

    The output of the room is artifacts: mocks, prototypes, fragments. Conversations happen around the work, not in lieu of it.

  2. Throughput beats polish.

    A loose pass on twenty ideas teaches more than a finished pass on one. Polish later, when the right idea has revealed itself.

  3. Kill fast, kill kindly.

    Most concepts die in the room. The team has stopped attaching ego to any single one — the value is in the next one.

Transcript Brief//Practice
11 / 20
A practice the CEO refuses to outsource

Talk to customers. Especially the ones the dashboard hides.

Dashboards measure what is. Conversations surface what isn’t there yet — the thing the user wanted but couldn’t articulate, the workaround they invented, the moment they almost churned.

Dashboards

Tell you what people did.

Aggregate behaviour. Lagging signals. Decisions you can defend in a slide.

Conversations

Tell you why they did it.

Latent intent. Workarounds. The product they wish existed. Decisions you can’t reach any other way.

Transcript Brief//Origin Story
12 / 20
Origin: Stories

The biggest format of the decade was a side effect.

Stories was not the headline feature. It was a by-product of solving a different problem — how to share something with a small audience without the weight of permanence. The team noticed users behaving as if the feature already existed; they shipped the surface around the behaviour.

Step 01

Solve a different problem

Ephemeral 1:1 sharing — the original Snapchat thesis.

Step 02

Watch the workarounds

Users sending the same snap to many friends, broadcast-style.

Step 03

Build the surface around the behaviour

A 24-hour public reel. Stories, as we now know it.

Step 04

Watch it eat the industry

Within five years, every major social app shipped a clone.

The micro-feature that saved early Snapchat

Trust is built in tiny places.

Ephemeral messaging falls apart the instant users feel surveilled. The fix wasn’t a feature roadmap — it was one notification.

Telling the sender when their snap was screenshotted made the asymmetry visible. It didn’t prevent the screenshot. It made trust possible anyway. A four-line decision that anchored a billion-user product.

Transcript Brief//PMs
14 / 20
PMs at Snap

For years, there weren’t any.

Snap operated for most of its first decade with designers and engineers carrying the product. PMs were added later, deliberately, into a culture that already knew how to ship without them.

Then — pre-PM era

Designers and engineers shipped the product.

  • No layer of translation between intent and artifact.
  • Designers owned UX, engineers owned the system.
  • Roadmap lived inside the design review.
  • The cost: scaling pains as the org grew past 100.

Now — what PMs actually do

Connective tissue. Not gatekeepers.

  • Hold the cross-functional thread (data, marketing, ops).
  • Name the trade-offs the design room can’t see.
  • Defend long-arc bets when the calendar gets crowded.
  • Earn their seat by accelerating shipping, not slowing it.

What Evan looks for

Three traits that survive the design review.

// Trait 01

Range

Can move from a wordmark to a hardware enclosure to a feed without losing the thread. The breadth is what makes a small team feasible.

// Trait 02

Restlessness

Can’t stop iterating. The portfolio is a record of revisions, not a museum of finished objects. Constructive dissatisfaction with their own work.

// Trait 03

Ships

Has put real things in front of real users. Has seen the gap between a comp in Figma and a feature in production — and crossed it.

Younger designers are paired with senior ones the same week they start. The fastest way to develop taste is proximity to it.

Transcript Brief//The Contrarian Take
18 / 20
The contrarian take

The AI conversation is dominated by capability — what the model can do, what it scores, when it surpasses us. Evan’s read is that the rate of adoption isn’t set by capability at all. It’s set by what people are emotionally and culturally willing to live with.

// 18 — Contrarian

Humanity is the bottleneck. Not the technology.

The next decade of AI won’t be gated by what the model can do. It’ll be gated by how fast society can absorb the change — and the pushback that comes with it.
