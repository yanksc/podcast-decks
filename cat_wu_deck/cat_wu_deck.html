The underrated AI skill

Ask the model to diagnose itself.

When a run goes sideways, most people retry. The leverage move is to hand the failure back to the model and ask why it failed — the introspection often produces the next prompt.

you » What went wrong on the last attempt? Where did your reasoning break down, and what would you do differently next time? » I misread the schema in step 2 and assumed a join key that didn’t exist. Next time I should verify the column names before generating the query — do you want me to retry with that check?

The model is often a better critic of its own work than you are. Use it.

The underrated discipline

Evals are the new specs.

If you can’t measure what “good” looks like, you can’t ship it. The teams that take evals seriously compound — everyone else just argues about vibes.

Writing evals forces a team to define the product before they build it. That alone is worth it. The fact that they also catch regressions is a bonus.

A good eval
is worth a thousand demos

Field Notes·Reliability
11 / 13
Reliability is the product

95 % automation isn’t good enough.

Demoware lives at 95. Real products live at 99. The last few percent is where the value — and most of the engineering — actually lives.

If users have to babysit, they don’t trust it. If they don’t trust it, they don’t hand over the work that justifies the price.

95%
demoware

The red sliver is where the trust is earned.

Field Notes·Practice
12 / 13
For PMs trying to break in

Build apps you actually use — not prototypes you abandon.

A demo proves you can prompt. A daily-driver app proves you understand the loop, the failure modes, and the edges. Hiring managers can tell the difference in five minutes.

The portfolio trap
A folder of half-finished prototypes.
Built for the screenshot. Never opened again.
What actually signals
One tool you use every day.
Fixed the bugs. Smoothed the edges. Knows where the model still falls down.
