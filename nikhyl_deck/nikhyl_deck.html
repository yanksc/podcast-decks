The Forecast
04 / 35
The forecast

The next two years will be the most chaotic period in product-management history.

Three things are changing at once: how companies build, what they need a PM to do, and what they’re willing to pay for. Most professions get one of those at a time. The PM industry is getting all three.

Warning

“Our industry is very much in stress. Nothing’s constant. Everyone’s in a state of alert.”

Layoffs, role redefinitions, comp resets, and a complete inversion of which skills companies pay for — all happening on top of each other. The chaos is the environment, not a phase to wait out.

The Split
06 / 35
Two archetypes diverge

For the first time, the PM job has cleanly split into two professions.

Three years ago we talked about “archetypes” the way you talk about flavors of the same role. That language no longer holds. The market is now hiring one of them and quietly retiring the other.

In trouble

The Information Mover

  • Day was largely moving information — status reports, doc relays, alignment loops.
  • Value came from being the connective tissue between functions.
  • Most of that work is now done faster and more honestly by an agent.
  • About half of the people who grew into PM have this shape.
In demand

The Builder

  • Got into product because they like making things, not managing them.
  • Comfortable in Codex / Claude Code / Lovable. Ships, doesn’t just plan.
  • The hires the survey is counting. The bidding war is on this side.
  • “Builders are going to have the time of their lives.”

The Decline
07 / 35
The dinosaur

The information mover is becoming a dinosaur.

If your value to the org was knowing what was happening across teams — running the status report, brokering the doc, summarizing the meeting — that work is now an agent call away. The role didn’t disappear; the moat around the role did.

Nikhyl is unsentimental about it: “That part of the job, frankly, just absolutely sucked.” The work was always lower-leverage than the people doing it wanted to admit. AI just made that legible to the C-suite.

The information mover is essentially going to become a dinosaur. — Nikhyl Singhal

The renaissance

The other half of the profession is having a renaissance.

Comp is up. Bay Area is back in favor. The bidding wars on senior product builders are loud and getting louder. Anyone who can open a laptop and ship — PM, designer, marketer, ops — is suddenly the most expensive person on the floor.

“Lots of engineers are builders, lots of designers are builders, lots of marketing folks are builders. Builders-wanted is going to be the big tagline for the next couple of years.”

The Skill
10 / 35
What “judgment” really means now

Deciding what to build, deciding if it’s good, deciding if it’s ready.

When the agent can write the doc, run the analysis, and draft the spec, what’s left for the PM is the part that was always the hardest and the most underpaid: taste. Saying yes to the right thing, no to the loud thing, and now to the slow thing.

The role didn’t shrink — the connective-tissue layer fell away and the judgment layer got more exposed.

Deciding what to build, deciding if this is good and great and ready, and prioritizing — that’s what remains. — Lenny & Nikhyl, on what survives

The Floor Rises
11 / 35
The floor rises

There won’t be any more bad software.

The bottom of the industry — clunky tools, half-baked CRUD apps, the long tail of internal stuff nobody loved — gets rebuilt by AI in months. The bar for “acceptable” jumps. Mediocre product gets replaced before it gets pitied.

Which means: the implicit deal where most PMs spent careers shipping “fine” products is gone. Fine isn’t a bar anyone’s paying for anymore.

The implication for PMs: you cannot earn a seat by shipping okay. The okay tier is a button now. You earn the seat by deciding what gets built in the first place — and by recognizing “great” when the model can produce thirty “goods” an hour.

Two PMs with the same job title can sit on opposite sides of the divide. The diagnostic isn’t the resume — it’s how the person spends their best hour of the day, and what kind of work makes them late for dinner.

The Hard Conversation
14 / 35
The hard conversation

What about the people who don’t want to build?

Nikhyl’s honest answer: there’s a class of PM who reads all of this and feels nothing. No spark. No curiosity. The work that excites the new market simply doesn’t excite them.

If you find the whole thing kind of boring, nauseating — you’re probably not in for the next version of our industry. And you should be honest with yourself. — Nikhyl Singhal

That’s not a moral judgment. It’s a compatibility test. The kindest thing for that person is to stop pretending and explore what does light them up — another function, another industry, another stage of life. The cruelest thing is to stay and resent the work.

Should PMs code?

Wrong question. The real one is: do you ship?

“Coding” is a red herring — the bar isn’t writing production C++. The bar is whether you can walk from idea to working artifact without handing the work to four other people first. Claude Code, Codex, Lovable — pick one and live in it.

The PM who used to swap in light bulbs for satisfaction can now ship the actual feature. That’s the whole shift.

Counter-current
16 / 35
A counter-current

Experienced leaders still matter — just not for the reasons they used to.

The narrative that AI flattens seniority is half-true. What flattens is the information arbitrage seniority used to confer. What deepens is the value of pattern recognition, calibrated judgment, and the willingness to make a call when the data is contradictory.

What experience used to buy

Networks. Knowing who to ask. Having seen the playbook. Defending the roadmap from a louder room.

What experience buys now

Calibration. Calling great from good. Holding the line when the model produces twenty plausible answers and only one of them is the right thing for the user.

The Setback
17 / 35
The setback nobody’s talking about

A diversity unwind is happening underneath the AI story.

When companies cut the “information mover” tier, the cuts disproportionately hit cohorts the industry spent a decade trying to bring in. The work moved to the “builder” tier, which got built before the industry diversified its pipelines.

The optimistic story — “AI will democratize building” — is true on a 5-year horizon. On a 24-month horizon, the people getting laid off and the people getting hired don’t look the same. That’s a real cost, and it’s being underweighted in industry conversation.

For leaders: hiring AI-first doesn’t have to mean hiring from one demographic. The actual signal — ships, builds, has taste — is broadly available. The lazy version of the search is the one that compounds the setback.

The Block
19 / 35
Why change is so hard for humans

The block isn’t the new skill. It’s the identity attached to the old one.

People aren’t avoiding AI tools because they can’t learn them. They’re avoiding them because using them well requires admitting that the version of the job they were great at is winding down.

That’s a grief problem dressed up as a productivity problem. The career-coaching question isn’t “which course should you take.” It’s “are you willing to be a beginner again, in public, for six months?”

Most people aren’t going to cross that mental threshold. And they’re going to be left behind by everybody else. — Nikhyl Singhal

The Trap
20 / 35
The trap mid-career people fall into

The “equal disappointment” algorithm.

Most senior PMs are running an unspoken optimization where they try to disappoint each constituency by exactly the same small amount — boss, partner, kids, team, self. The work product is “adequate.” Nobody’s yelling. Nobody’s thrilled either.

boss=partner=kids=self → everyone equally let down, all the time

It’s a stable equilibrium, and it’s exactly the equilibrium AI is breaking. The market is now paying for people who pick a constituency to fully disappoint — and crush the one that’s left.

The chaos has a shape — and it ends.

Nikhyl’s arc: the next 18–24 months are the storm. After that, a new equilibrium settles in, with new norms about who does what and how much it pays. The window to position yourself is now — not after.

The Prescription
24 / 35
The prescription

Find your moment of joy. Then chase it.

Nikhyl’s single most important career advice for the next two years isn’t about strategy. It’s about noticing. The moment something clicks — you build a small thing with the model and time disappears — that’s the signal. Most people feel it once and don’t recognize it.

“Joy is the biggest antidote to burnout.”

The moment you have joy, the moment it doesn’t feel like work. Most product management feels like work if you’re not building. We’re moving into a world where it can feel like building — and joy — not work. You just need to find the path to get there.

Practical: an app on the side. A weekend prototype for a problem you have. A Claude session in the background while you watch TV. The point isn’t the project — the point is catching the bug.

The Action List
26 / 35
Specific advice, this week

If you do six things, you’ll be in the rehired 8,000.

  1. Open Claude Code or Codex tonight.

    Not tomorrow, not next week. Build something terrible. Cross the threshold from spectator to participant.

  2. Pick a personal problem and ship a tool for it.

    Not a portfolio piece — a thing you actually use. Daily-driver beats demoware on every resume now.

  3. Replace one weekly status doc with an agent.

    If you’re still hand-assembling reports, you’re training the org to value information-moving over building.

  4. Stop optimizing for equal disappointment.

    Pick one constituency to disappoint deliberately for the next 90 days. Use the recovered hours to build.

  5. Refresh your portfolio with artifacts, not affiliations.

    Two screenshots and a working link beat three logos. The interview is about what you made, not where you sat.

  6. Notice the moment something clicks. Chase it.

    The first time time disappears, that’s the signal. Plan the next week around producing more of those moments.

The Re-Shape
28 / 35
Alignment

Alignment doesn’t go away. It just gets harder — and more valuable.

A lot of “alignment” was just information shuffling: getting people the ground truth so they could stop fighting about facts. The CEO can now ask the agent and have the ground truth in seconds.

What’s left is the harder thing: opinion under disagreement. Deciding what to fight for when everyone has the same data. That part of alignment isn’t shrinking — it’s the whole job.

A lot of alignment was getting people the ground level truth. That problem is dramatically better. Now someone has to decide and have an opinion and what to fight for. — Nikhyl Singhal

The Bigger Story
29 / 35
The bigger story most PMs are missing

Engineering is changing more than PM is.

PMs are loud about their own disruption. The reality is that the engineering profession is being reshaped harder and faster — junior engineering work is collapsing into agent prompts, and senior engineers are being asked to act more like product builders than ever.

Implication for PMs: your engineering counterparts are also in a chaotic skill flip. The window to pair with them on shipped artifacts is open right now — before either side hardens into the new shape and forgets how to collaborate across the line.

The Surprise
30 / 35
The surprise

Design has hit a quiet plateau.

Counter-intuitively, AI hasn’t supercharged the design profession the way it has engineering. The output of generative tools is improving fast, but the bar for “cohesive, on-brand, shippable” isn’t closing as quickly as people predicted.

For PMs: don’t assume your designer is automatable. The opposite, in fact — the designers who’ve crossed into shipping code with taste are some of the most expensive humans in tech this year.

The Tempo
31 / 35
The tempo of the next two years

You have to find the fire in the belly — or borrow it.

Nikhyl’s most blunt advice: pace is no longer optional. The reserve you held back during steady-state years is the reserve you spend now. The professionals who get rehired in the 8,000 are people who showed up with a different gear.

You have to find the ability to increase pace. You’ve got to find that reserve. The next two years require a lot of fire in the belly. — Nikhyl Singhal

That doesn’t mean grinding harder at the wrong things. It means matching the metabolism of the moment — shipping faster, learning faster, deciding faster — which the people on the joy side of the threshold do almost without trying.

The Reframe
32 / 35
People have more energy than they realize. They’re just so exhausted by the monotony of what was defined as product management. — Nikhyl Singhal
A reframe

The exhaustion is from the old shape, not from the work itself.

A surprising read from Nikhyl’s coaching practice: senior PMs are not, mostly, burnt out from too much work. They’re burnt out from the wrong work — the same status meeting, the same alignment loop, the same political defense of the same roadmap.

When the same people get a chance to build, the energy is suddenly there. The reservoir was never empty. The activity it was being spent on was the problem.
