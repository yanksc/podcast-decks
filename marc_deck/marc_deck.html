The guest

A builder-investor who thinks in centuries.

Marc has watched and shaped the consumer-internet era from the browser forward. He frames AI not as a product cycle but as a civilizational arrival — and one whose timing, he argues, is almost suspiciously good.

His read on the present moment leans heavily on long horizons: demographic curves, technological waves, the historical record of who wins what kind of disruption.

The Almanac · The Moment
III / XXXV
The moment we live in

A very, very historic time — and most people are pricing it as a quarter, not a century.

Marc’s opening frame is the largest one available: this isn’t a product cycle, a tech wave, or even a decade-long shift. It is, in his telling, a once-in-civilization arrival of a tool that converts the most abundant material on earth (sand, made into silicon) into the most rare (thought).

AI is the philosopher’s stone. We have a technology that transfers the most common thing in the world — sand — into the most rare thing in the world: thought. — Marc Andreessen

The Almanac · A Reframe
VI / XXXV
A reframe

Stop looking at job loss. Start looking at task loss.

The single most important conceptual shift in the conversation. Jobs are bundles of tasks. Tasks get automated long before jobs do — and when tasks leave, the job often becomes more valuable, not less, because the human has time for the part that mattered.

The bookkeeper didn’t disappear when the spreadsheet arrived. The bookkeeper became an accountant.

Everybody wants to talk about job loss. But really what you want to look at is task loss. The job persists longer than the individual tasks. — Marc Andreessen

The Almanac · The Panic
VII / XXXV
The panic

The mass-unemployment scenario is totally off base.

Marc’s flat read on the “AI takes everyone’s jobs” narrative. Not because AI isn’t powerful, but because the labor pool the developed world has — and is going to have — is shrinking faster than the technology can replace it.

The panic gets the demographics backwards. Workers will be at a premium, not a discount. The bottleneck for the next two decades is going to be the absence of humans, not the presence of machines.

That doesn’t mean no displacement. It means the macro story is the inverse of the headline: a widening gap between what economies need to produce and the labor pool available, with AI partially — not entirely — closing it.

The Almanac · Three Roles, One Triangle
IX / XXXV
The Mexican standoff

PM, designer, engineer — each thinks they can become the other two.

Marc’s sharpest observation about the present: every coder believes AI lets them also be a PM and a designer. Every PM thinks they can be a coder and a designer. Every designer knows they can be a PM and a coder.

And here’s the twist: they’re all correct. The standoff isn’t about who wins — it’s that the line between the three roles is dissolving for anyone willing to cross it.

“I can do PM & design”

“I can code & design”

The Almanac · The Standoff, Continued
X / XXXV
The standoff is not just in tech

Hollywood’s version: director, writer, actor — same triangle, same dissolve.

Marc’s lateral move that lights up the framework. The director thinks AI can write the script and play the parts. The writer thinks AI can direct and cast. The actor thinks AI can write and direct, and they’ll just turn up and perform.

The pattern is general. Wherever a creative discipline used to require three specialized humans handing off to each other, the dissolve is happening — and the standoff is the result. Whoever crosses two of the three lines first owns the room.

Look for the triangle in your industry. There almost certainly is one. The question is which corner you’re standing in — and which other corner you’re willing to learn.

The Almanac · The Insight
XI / XXXV
The math under the standoff

Why combining skills is so disproportionately rewarded. The arithmetic Marc keeps coming back to — borrowed from Scott Adams — explains why the “learn one more thing” advice keeps compounding.

XI — The arithmetic

Two skills > double. Three skills > triple.

The additive effect of being good at two things is more than double the effect of either one. The additive effect of three is more than triple. The combination is where the value lives.

The Almanac The arithmetic
The Almanac · An Illustration
XII / XXXV
An illustration: how the math creates Dilbert

Scott Adams was a pretty good cartoonist and a pretty good businessman. The combination made him spectacular.

Marc’s favorite worked example. Adams himself put it this way: the world’s best cartoonist who didn’t understand business could not have made Dilbert. The world’s best businessperson who couldn’t draw could not have made Dilbert. It took someone with both — even at a moderate level of each — to produce one of the most successful comic strips ever printed.

The same logic produces Hollywood’s “auteur” — the writer-director who is uncopyable because the combination is what they are.

Career shape

From T-shaped to E-shaped.

The T-shaped career — one deep skill, broad collaboration — was the right shape for the last decade. The E-shaped career — one deep skill, two or three serious adjacent ones, plus AI as a force multiplier on all of them — is the right shape for the next.

AI doesn’t make the deep specialty optional. It makes the second and third specialties suddenly accessible.

The Almanac · The Career Advice
XIV / XXXV
The career advice he keeps coming back to

Marc’s shortest piece of career advice. It compresses the additive-skill argument, the standoff, and the E-shaped career into three words. If you do nothing else with this conversation, do this.

XIV — The advice

Don’t be fungible.

If you can be replaced by anyone with your title, you will be. The work of a career is the work of becoming a combination that doesn’t exist anywhere else — and AI is the most powerful tool we’ve ever had for assembling that combination.

Yes, you should still learn to code.

The take that surprises people. AI doesn’t make coding obsolete — it makes coding more accessible and more leveraged. The instinct to read the code, fix it, ship it, and reason about systems is what separates people who direct AI from people who get directed by it.

The Almanac · Reading the Machine
XVI / XXXV
Why understanding code still matters

The job is no longer writing code. It’s reading it — with judgment.

When the model writes the function, the leverage is in the human who can look at the output and say “this is wrong, here’s why, here’s the cleaner approach.” That’s a code-literacy skill, not a typing skill.

The people who refuse to read code in the AI era are putting themselves in the same position non-readers were in during the Reformation: trusting an intermediary they have no way to check.

People who really want to improve themselves and develop their careers should be spending every spare hour right now talking to AI: “train me up.” — Marc Andreessen

The Almanac · Design
XVII / XXXV
Design in the AI era

Designers don’t lose value. They finally get to the higher work.

Marc’s read on the design profession is more optimistic than most. The boring 70% of design work — producing the assets, exploring permutations, polishing the obvious — is what AI is best at. Which means the designer’s time can move up the stack to the part most never reach.

  1. Strategy — what should this even be?

    The framing of the product, the principles of the brand, the answer to the question users haven’t articulated.

  2. Taste — calling great from good.

    When the model produces 30 plausible options per minute, the value isn’t producing them. It’s knowing which one is the right one.

  3. Cohesion — making the whole thing one thing.

    Every surface produced by AI in isolation drifts. Designers become the cohesion gate — the only role that holds the product together as a single artifact.

The Almanac · On Children
XVIII / XXXV
On raising children for this

Marc raised his ten-year-old to treat AI as a tutor, not a search engine.

The mental model he’s installed in his son: AI is the patient tutor every wealthy family used to hire, now available to anyone with curiosity. The skill is asking, listening, asking again. The skill is not Googling.

Bloom’s 2-sigma problem — finally solvable.

In 1984, Bloom showed that students with one-on-one tutors performed two standard deviations better than those in classrooms. The catch: tutors don’t scale. AI is the first technology that can deliver one-on-one tutoring at population scale. The implications for education are civilizational.

The Almanac · On Moats
XXI / XXXV
A live debate inside venture

Where do the moats actually live in the AI era?

Marc’s honest answer is “we’re still figuring it out.” The debate inside a16z — and across his peers — is alive. Three candidate answers keep surfacing, all imperfect, none fully resolved.

  1. Distribution.

    Whoever owns the channel wins the category. The argument that’s strongest in consumer.

  2. Data — the proprietary kind.

    Unique signals nobody else can replicate at any price. Strongest in regulated industries, financial services, healthcare.

  3. Compounding workflows.

    Products that get more useful the longer a customer uses them, with switching costs growing weekly. Strongest in enterprise.

The Almanac · The Pace
XXII / XXXV
The pace

Model improvement still surprises even the people building them.

The cadence of capability jumps hasn’t cooled. Each generation reaches benchmarks the previous one couldn’t touch. For products built on top, the design problem is no longer “what works today?” — it’s “what will work in eight months?”

The implication for builders: build the version of the product that doesn’t quite work yet. By the time you ship it, the model has caught up — and you’re ahead of every competitor who built only what was possible at the moment they started.

This is the design discipline of the era: imagining the artifact for a model that doesn’t exist for another six months — and shipping it now anyway.

The Almanac · A Disposition
XXIII / XXXV
A disposition for VC — and life

Indeterminate optimism.

Borrowed from Peter Thiel’s framework but turned into Marc’s personal stance. The future is going to be brighter than the present — and we don’t know specifically how. That second clause is the one most people skip.

The discipline is to invest, build, and live as though good things are coming, while staying honest that the specific shape of those good things will keep surprising you. The opposite of cynicism. Also the opposite of utopian planning.

Indeterminate optimism — the future is going to be better, and we don’t know exactly how. That uncertainty is a feature, not a bug. — Marc Andreessen, paraphrased

The Almanac · On AGI
XXIV / XXXV
On AGI

If we never define it precisely, does it matter when we get there?

Marc’s wry framing on the AGI debate. Every benchmark we set gets cleared, then redefined the next year. At some point you have to ask whether the moving target is doing useful work — or whether the goalposts are moving because the conversation needs them to.

His pragmatic stance: stop trying to define the line. Watch what the systems do. Watch what they don’t do. Watch what they replace and what they create around themselves. The threshold will be obvious in the rear-view mirror — the way the Industrial Revolution was.

The Almanac · A Diet
XXV / XXXV
Marc’s media diet

X and old books. Nothing in between.

A pragmatic two-channel diet that surprises people. The signal he wants from the present is fastest on X. The signal he wants about everything else — human nature, civilizations, decisions, mistakes — is slowest in books written before 1950.

The logic: the present is moving so fast that anything in between — weekly magazines, monthly think-pieces, prestige newsletters — arrives stale on one end and shallow on the other. The bookend strategy maximizes both speed and depth.

Worth stress-testing your own diet against this. How much of what you read is fast enough to inform action? How much is deep enough to inform judgment? Most diets deliver neither.

The Almanac · Practical Adjustments
XXVI / XXXV
Practical adjustments inside an existing job

Watch what shifts in your week. Reallocate to the part AI can’t do.

For people who don’t want to change careers but want to keep their seat, Marc’s practical guidance is unglamorous: track which tasks the model takes from you, and deliberately move your attention up the stack.

  1. Audit your week.

    Which 8 hours could a competent agent do this Friday? Move them. Use the recovered hours for the work that actually requires you.

  2. Pick the up-stack work.

    Strategy. Taste. Cross-functional alignment. Customer conversations. The work that the model is genuinely worse at.

  3. Repeat quarterly.

    The frontier moves. What was up-stack last quarter is in-frontier this one. The audit is recurring, not one-time.

The Almanac Adapting tasks
The Almanac · A Hint from History
XXVII / XXXV
A hint from the last shift

When code went from C to Python, the population of programmers exploded.

Marc’s pattern from the previous wave. Each move “up” the abstraction stack — assembly to C to Java to Python to JavaScript — multiplied the number of people who could meaningfully build software. The current move (English as a programming language) is the largest jump yet.

If history rhymes, the population of people who can build software in 2030 won’t be 30 million. It’ll be hundreds of millions. The competitive landscape that produces is different in kind, not degree.

The Almanac · Labor Markets
XXVIII / XXXV
Labor markets, repriced

Remaining human workers will be at a premium, not a discount.

The headline of Marc’s economic argument. Combine declining birth rates with rising productivity from AI, and the market for the humans still in the labor force tightens. Compensation goes up. Bargaining power goes up. The narrative that AI “commoditizes” workers reads it backwards.

The remaining human workers are going to be at a premium, not at a discount. — Marc Andreessen

The Almanac · Org Theory
XXIX / XXXV
A new theory of the firm

Companies are bundles of decisions — not bundles of headcount.

An implication of the additive-skill argument applied at the org level. The valuable thing about a company isn’t how many people it employs — it’s the rate and quality of decisions it produces. Smaller, denser teams making sharper decisions, leveraged by AI, will outperform large slow ones at almost every scale.

This rewires hiring, organizational design, M&A, valuation, and the very definition of company size. The Industrial Revolution’s dominant unit was the factory. The AI era’s dominant unit may be the small studio.

The Almanac · Interfaces
XXX / XXXV
Interfaces, finally evolving

Voice is going to matter again — and this time it works.

Marc has watched voice interfaces fail in three previous cycles. The current one is different: the latency, the prosody, the ability to interrupt and be interrupted have crossed the threshold where conversation feels like conversation rather than transaction.

For products: the design space just got bigger. The set of contexts where the right interface is “talk to it” rather than “tap on it” is going to surprise people who haven’t built for voice in fifteen years.

The Almanac · The Risk
XXXII / XXXV
The risk on the other side

The danger isn’t AI taking your job. It’s refusing to pick it up.

Marc’s reframe of the panic. The vast majority of people displaced in this cycle won’t be displaced by the technology — they’ll be displaced by other people who used the technology and reorganized their work around it first.

The asymmetry is brutal but accessible: the cost of starting now is small. The cost of starting in 2028 is enormous.

If you’ve been postponing the “learn AI” week on your calendar — this is a sign. The window is open and small.
